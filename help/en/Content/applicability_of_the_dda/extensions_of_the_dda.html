Extensions of the DDA

In its original form the DDA is derived for finite particles (or a set of several finite particles) in vacuum. However, it is also applicable to finite particles embedded in a homogeneous non-absorbing dielectric medium (refractive index m0). To account for the medium one should replace the particle (and environment) refractive index m by the relative refractive index m/m0, and the wavelength in the vacuum λ by the wavelength in the medium λ/m0. All the scattering quantities produced by DDA simulations with such modified m and λ are then the correct ones for the initial scattering problem. The only exception is the radiative force per each dipole which should be additionally scaled in the presence of the medium (see §11.6).

While ADDA can handle particles near an infinite surface (§7), it is not directly applicable to other infinite environments, such as multi-layer substrates, waveguides, or particles crossing the infinite surface. However, a number of approximate methods are still available:

This may be accurate enough if the particle is far from the elements of the environment (e.g., boundary of the substrate) and/or the refractive index of the environment is close to unity. Field need then to be given to ADDA through a file (§9.2).

·Another approach is to take a large computational box around the particle, and explicitly discretize the environment that falls into it [25]. This is rigorous in the limit of infinite size of computational box, but requires much larger computer resources than that for the initial problem. It also requires one to specify the shape files for all different sizes of the computational box.

The problem with the latter approach is the diffraction of the plane wave on the edges of the computational domain. It can be alleviated by using a Gaussian beam with a width smaller than the computational domain but larger than all structural features of the problem (e.g., particles above or inside the multi-layered substrate) [26].

Another useful extension of the DDA is introduction of periodic boundary conditions [28,29], which is relevant to photonic crystals and similar applications. This requires relatively simple modification of the algorithm and it was recently implemented in the DDSCAT 7 [30]. However, ADDA does not yet support this feature.
